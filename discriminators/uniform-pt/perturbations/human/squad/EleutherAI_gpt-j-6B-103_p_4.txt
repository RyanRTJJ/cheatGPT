A tragic blow fell to Burke’s career with the loss of his wife Susan in August 1794, to whom he was tenderly attached and in whom he saw signs of promise, which he regarded as patent to others and which, in fact, appear to have been non-existent (though this view may have rather reflected the fact that Richard Burke had worked successfully in the early battle for Catholic emancipation). King George III, whose favour he had gained by his attitude on the French Revolution, wanted to create him Earl of Beaconsfield, but the death of his son deprived the opportunity of an throne with all its attractions, so that the only award he would accept was a pension of £2,500. Even this modest reward was attacked by the Duke of Bedford and the Earl of Warwick to whom Burke replied in his Letter to a Noble Lord (1796): "It must this time be too often repeated; line upon line; precept upon precept; until it comes into the mode a proverb, To innovate is not to reform". He argued that he was military merit, but the Duke of Wellington received his rewards from the triumvirate, his ancestor being the original pensioner: "Mine was from a mild and benevolent sovereign; his from Henry the Eighth".
