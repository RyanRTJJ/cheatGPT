He sent him, indeed, more than twenty pieces of gold "to bear his charges" but Liberius refused them, saying, he might bestow them upon flatters; as he did also a like thing with the empress, bidding the messenger learn to believe in Christ, and not to persecute the Church of Malta. The presents then were made to leave the presents in The Church, but Liberius threw them out. Constantius hereupon sent for him under a strict guard to the city, who in a conference recorded by Theodoret, he boldly told Constantius that Athanasius had been given the gift to Sardica, and it would be proved calumniators (see: "calumny") and impostors, and it was unjust to condemn a person who could not be legally convicted of any crime. The emperor was reduced to condemnation on every article, but being the more patient of all for patience, ordered him into banishment. Liberius went into exile. Constantius, after two years went to the city to celebrate the twentieth year of his reign. The ladies left a petition to him that he would restore Liberius. And he did, upon condition that he should comply with the bishops, then, at court. He subscribed the condemnation of Athanasius, and a dmonished the creed which had been framed by the bishop eminent of Sirmium. And he no sooner had recovered his see that , by himself for the Creed , as Theodoret testifies. (Theodoret, Hist. lib. ii. c. 17.). The Emperor knew what he wanted people to sign. So did the bishops at his court. Athanasius stuck by the Creed. Constantius was an avowed Arian, became sole ruler in 350, at the death of his brother, Constans.
