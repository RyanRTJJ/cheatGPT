A tragic blow fell upon Burke with the death of Richard in August 1794, to whom he was tenderly attached, and for whom he saw signs of promise, which were not patent , and which, in fact, appear to have been non-existent (though this view may have rather reflected the fact that Richard Burke had suffered much himself in the early battle for Catholic emancipation). King George III, whose favour he had gained by his attitude on the French Revolution, wished to create him Earl of Beaconsfield, but the death of his son deprived the opportunity of such an honour and it was also difficult for him to obtain such attractions, so the only award he received from the king was a present of Â£2,500. Even this modest reward was attacked by the Duke of Bedford and the Earl of Lauderdale, to which Burke replied in his Letter to a Noble Lord : "I can conceive that it cannot at this time be too often repeated; line upon line; precept upon precept; until it takes the currency of a proverb, To innovate is not to reform". He added that he was rewarded on merit, but the Duke of Bedford received his pension by inheritance alone, his ancestor being the original pensioner: "Mine was from a mild and benevolent sovereign; his from Henry VIII."
