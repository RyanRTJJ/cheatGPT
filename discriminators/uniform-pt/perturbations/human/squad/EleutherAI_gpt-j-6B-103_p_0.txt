A tragic blow fell upon Burke with the loss of Richard in August 1794, to whom he was tenderly attached, and in whom he saw great promise, which were not patent to others but, in fact, appear to have been non-existent (though this view may have rather reflected the fact that Richard had worked successfully in the battle for Catholic emancipation). King George III, whose favour he had gained by his attitude on the French Revolution, wished to create him Earl of Beaconsfield, but the death of his son deprived the opportunity of this honour of any of its attractions, so that the only award he would accept was a pension of Â£2,500. Even this modest reward was attacked by the Duke of Bedford and the Earl of Lauderdale, to which he replied in his Letter to a Noble Friend that "It cannot at this time be too often repeated; line upon line; precept upon precept; that innovation comes into the currency of a nation where to innovate is not to reform". He argued that he was rewarded on merit, but the Duke of Bedford received his rewards from inheritance alone, his ancestor . He compared this with the original pensioner: "Mine was from a mild and benevolent sovereign; from Henry the Eighth".
