A tragic blow fell upon Burke with the loss of Richard in August 1794, to whom he was tenderly attached, and in whom he saw signs of improvement, which were not patent to others , and which, in fact, appear to have been non-existent (though this may have rather reflected the fact that Richard Burke had worked successfully in the early battle for Catholic emancipation). King George III, whose favour he had gained through his attitude on the French Revolution, wished to make him Earl of Beaconsfield, but the death of his son deprived the opportunity of the honour and all its attractions, so the only award he would accept was a sum of Â£2,500. Even this modest reward was attacked by the Duke of Bedford and the Earl of Lauderdale, to whom Burke replied in his Letter to a Noble Lord (1796): "It cannot at this time be too often repeated; line upon line; precept upon precept, till it comes into the currency of the Nation. To innovate is not to be born". He was not rewarded, he was rewarded on merit, but the Duke of Bedford received his rewards from inheritance alone, his reward "from the original royal house of Marlborough, and the grant was from a mild and benevolent bequest from Henry the Eighth".
