A heavy loss fell upon Burke with the loss of Richard in August 1794, to whom he was tenderly attached, and in whom there were signs of promise, which were not patent to others and which, in Burke's mind, are supposed to have been non-existent (though this may have rather reflected the fact that Richard Burke had worked successfully in the early battle for Catholic emancipation). King George III, whose favour he had gained by his attitude on the French Revolution, wished to create him Earl of Cornwall. The Earl refused. But the death of his son deprived the opportunity of such an honour and all its complications; the only payment he would accept was a salary of Â£2,500. Even this modest reward was attacked by the Duke of Bedford and the Earl of Northampton, to whom Burke replied in his Letter to the Lord (1796): "It cannot at this time be too often repeated; line upon line; precept upon precept; until it comes into the mode of a proverb, To innovate is not to reform". He argued that he was rewarded on merit, but the Duke of Bedford received his rewards from inheritance ; his ancestor being the original Duke of York.
