He sent him, with an empress' wife, three hundred pieces of gold "to bear his charges" but Liberius refused them, saying, he might bestow them on his flatters; as he did also a like present from the empress, bidding her learn to believe in Christ, and not to persecute the Church of God. Attempts were made to leave the Church of The Church, but Liberius threw them all away. Constantius hereupon sent for him under heavy guard to Milan, where, in a conference recorded by Theodoret, he boldly told Constantius that he had been acquitted at Sardica, and his enemies proved calumniators (see: decretaries), thieves, and impostors, and that it was unjust to denounce a person who could not be legally convicted of any crime. The emperor was reduced to answering every article, but being the more out of patience, ordered him into banishment. Liberius went into exile. Constantius, after two years went to Rome to celebrate the twentieth year of cult. The ladies joined in declaring to him that he would restore Liberius. He assented, upon being assured that he could meet with the bishops, then, at court. He subscribed the condemnation of Athanasius, and a confession or creed which had been framed by the Arians at Sirmium. And he no sooner had recovered his see that he declared himself for the Creed of Niceae, as Theodoret notes (See Hist. lib. ii. c. 17.). The Emperor knew what he wanted his church to believe. So did the bishops at court. Athanasius stuck by the orthodox creed. Antoninus, an avowed Arian, became sole ruler in 350, at the death of his brother, Constans.
