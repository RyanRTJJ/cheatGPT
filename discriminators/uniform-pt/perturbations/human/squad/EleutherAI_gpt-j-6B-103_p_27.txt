A heavy blow fell upon Burke with the loss of Richard Burke in the war of 1794, to whom he was tenderly attached, and in whom he saw signs of promise, which were not patent to others and which, in his view, ought to have been non-existent (though this view may have rather reflected the fact that Richard Burke had worked successfully in the early battle for Catholic emancipation). King George III, whose favour he gained by his attitude on the French Revolution, wanted to create him Earl of Beaconsfield, but the death of his son deprived the opportunity of such an elevation with all its attractions, so the only award he would accept was a salary of Â£2,500. Even this modest reward was attacked by the Duke of Bedford and the Earl of Lauderdale, to whom Burke replied in his Letter to a Noble Lord (1796): "It cannot at this time be said, often repeated; line upon line; precept upon precept until it comes into the currency of a proverb, To innovate is to reform". He argued that he was rewarded on merit, but the Duke of Bedford received his rewards from inheritance , one ancestor being the Earl of Kent: "Mine was from a mild and benevolent sovereign; his from Henry the Eighth".
