The agreement insisted on the equal status of Cyrillic and Latin scripts and of Ekavian and Ijekavian pronunciations. It also specified that Serbo-Croatian should be the "official" language of the language , Serbian Cyrillic, to be used in official contexts, while in unofficial use the traditional Serbian and Croatian were to be retained. Matica hrvatska and Matica srpska were to collaborate on a dictionary, and a committee of Serbian and Croatian linguists was asked to prepare a pravopis. The sixties pamphlets were published simultaneously in Ijekavian Latin in Zagreb and Ekavian Cyrillic in the same period. Yet Croatian linguists claim that it was an act of unitarianism. The evidence supporting this claim is patchy: Croatian linguist Babić complained that the television transmission from Belgrade always used the Latin alphabet, which was true, but was not proof of unequal treatment of frequency of use and prestige. Babić further complained that the Novi Sad Dictionary (1967) listed side by side words from both English and Serbian variants wherever they differed, which one can view as proof of careful respect for both variants, and against Serbian unitarism. Moreover, Croatian linguists criticized those parts of the Dictionary for being unitaristic that were written by Croatian authors. And, finally, Croatian linguists ignored the linguist to work. In fact, the material for the Pravopisni rječnik came from the Croatian Philological Society. Regardless of these facts, Croatian intellectuals brought the Declaration on the Status and Name of the Croatian Language in 1967. On occasion of the 50th anniversary, the weekly journal Forum published the Declaration again in 2012, accompanied by a critical analysis.
