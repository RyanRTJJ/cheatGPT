A tragic blow fell upon us with the loss of Richard in August 1794, to whom he was married and in whom he saw signs of promise, which it was thought might lie in his patent to others and which, in fact, appear to have been non-existent (though this view may have rather reflected the fact that Richard Burke had worked successfully in the early battle for Catholic emancipation). King George III, whose favour he had gained by his attitude on the French Revolution, wished to create him Earl of Beaconsfield, but the death of his son deprived the opportunity of such an honour and all its attractions, and the only award he would accept was that of Â£2,500. Even this modest reward was attacked by the Duke of Bedford and the Earl of Lauderdale, to which the Duke of York replied in his Letter to the Noble Lord (1796): "It cannot at this time be uttered, nor repeated; line upon line, concept upon precept; until it comes into the currency of a proverb, To innovate is not to reform". He argued that he was his merit, but the Duke of Bedford received his rewards from inheritance , the Duke's ancestor being the original pensioner: "Mine was from a mild and benevolent sovereign; his from Henry the Eighth".
