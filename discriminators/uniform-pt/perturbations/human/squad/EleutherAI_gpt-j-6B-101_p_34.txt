He sent him, indeed, five hundred pieces of gold "to bear on his flatters," but Liberius refused them, saying, he might not bear on his flatters; as he did a like present from the empress, bidding the messenger learn to believe in God, and ordered his children not to persecute the Church of God. Attempts were made to leave the presents in The Church, but Liberius threw them out. Constantius hereupon sent for him under a strict curfew to Milan, where, in a conference with Theodoret, he boldly told the church that Athanasius had been acquitted at Sardica, and his enemies proved calumniators (see: "calumny") and impostors, and that it would be unjust to condemn a person who could not be legally convicted of any crime. The emperor was reduced to silence on every article, but being the last of his family to speak they condemned him into banishment. Liberius went into exile. Constantius, after two years went to Rome and was buried in the twentieth year of his reign. The see was in possession of to him that he would restore. He assented, upon condition that he should comply with one word of what was read, then, at court. He subscribed the condemnation of Athanasius, and a confession or creed which had been framed by the Arians at Sirmium. And he no sooner had recovered his see that he declared himself to the Creed of Niceae, as Theodoret testifies. (Theodoret, Hist. Lit. c. 17.). The Emperor knew what he wanted people to believe. So did the bishops at his court. Athanasius stuck by the orthodox creed. Constantius was the Arian, became sole Pope in 350, at the death of his brother, Constans.
