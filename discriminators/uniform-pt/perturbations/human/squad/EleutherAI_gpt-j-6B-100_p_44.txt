Under Dr John Hewson, in November 1991, the opposition launched the 650-page Fightback! policy document − a radical collection of "dry", no-action measures including the introduction of a Goods and Services Tax (GST), various changes to Medicare including the abolition of electronic billing for non-concession holders, the introduction of a new 'common measure' on unemployment benefits, various changes to industrial relations including the abolition of awards, a $13 billion personal income tax cut directed at middle and upper income earners, $10 billion in government spending cuts, the introduction of two new sales taxes and the privatisation of a huge amount of government owned enterprises − representing the start of a very different future direction to the keynesian economic conservatism practiced by previous Liberal/National governments. The 15 percent GST was the centerpiece of the policy . In early 1992, Labor Prime Minister Paul Keating launched a campaign against the government and particularly against the GST, which he described as an attack on the working class in that it shifted the tax burden from direct taxation of the wealthy to indirect taxation, which would be applied as a broad-based consumption tax. Pressure group activity and public opinion was relentless, which led to the legislation to exempt food from the proposed tax leading to questions surrounding the complexity of what food was and wasn't to be exempt from the GST. Hewson's difficulty in explaining what the tax was to the electorate was exemplified in the infamous birthday cake interview, considered by many as a turning point in the election campaign.
