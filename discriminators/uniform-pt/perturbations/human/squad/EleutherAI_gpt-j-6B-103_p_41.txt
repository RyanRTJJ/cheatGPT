A tragic blow fell upon Burke with the loss of Richard in August 1794, to whom he was close and in whom he saw signs of promise, which were not patent to others and , in fact, appear to have been non-existent (though this may have rather reflected the fact that Richard Burke worked successfully in the early battle for Catholic emancipation). King George III, whose favour he had gained by his attitude on the War, had wished to create him bishop of Beaconsfield, but the death of his son deprived the opportunity of such an honour and all its attractions, so the only thing Beaconsfield would accept was a pension . Even this modest reward was attacked by the Prince of Bedford and the Earl of Lauderdale, to whom Burke replied in his Letter to a Noble Lord (1796): "It cannot at once be too often repeated; line upon line; precept upon precept; until it comes into the currency of a proverb, To innovate is not to reform". He regretted it. Thence he was rewarded on merit, but the Duke of Bedford received his pension from inheritance alone, his ancestor being the original pensioner: "Mine was from a mild and benevolent sovereign; and thus, as it were, from that gentle King Henry the Eighth".
