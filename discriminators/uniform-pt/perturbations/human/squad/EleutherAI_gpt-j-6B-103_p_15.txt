A tragic blow fell upon Burke with the death of daughter Richard in August 1794, to whom he was tenderly attached, and in whom he saw signs of promise, but not patent to be the case which, in fact, appear to have been non-existent (though this view may have rather reflected the view of the time). Prior to his separation from Richard Burke had worked successfully in the early years of Catholic emancipation). King George III, whose favour he had gained by his attitude on the French Revolution, expected him to be made Earl of Beaconsfield, but the death of his son deprived the opportunity of such an honour and all its attractions, so the only consideration he would accept was a pension of Â£2,500. Even this modest reward was attacked by the Duke of Bedford , Earl of Lauderdale, to whom Burke replied in his Letter to a Noble Lord (1796): "It cannot for the first time be repeated, while it is not all repeated; line upon line; precept upon precept; until it come into the currency of a proverb, To innovate is not to reform". He argued that he was rewarded on merit, but the Duke of Bedford received his rewards from inheritance alone, his ancestor being the original pensioner: "Mine was from Henry the mild and benevolent sovereign; his from Henry the mild."
