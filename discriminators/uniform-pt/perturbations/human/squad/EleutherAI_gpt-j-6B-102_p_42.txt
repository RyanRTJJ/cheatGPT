The first formal school on American Indian land was held at Fort Owen in the Bitterroot valley in 1862. The students were Indian children and the children of Fort Owen employees. The first school term started . School started this winter and only lasted until February 28. Classes were taught by Mr. Robinson. Another early subscription school was started by Thomas O. Cosby in Virginia City in 1863. In this school, students were charged $1.75 per week. The Montana Territorial Legislative Assembly had its inaugural meeting in 1864. The first legislature authorized counties to levy taxes for schools, which set the foundations for public education in Virginia City. Hancock County was the first to take advantage of the newly authorized taxes and it formed fhe first public school in Virginia City in 1886. The first school year was supposed to begin in January 1866, but severe weather postponed its opening until March. The first school year ran through the summer and didn't end until August 17. One of the first teachers who helped with the school was Margaret Raymond. She was a 25-year-old woman who had traveled to Virginia City via wagon train from Washington, D.C. in 1865. To become a certified teacher, Raymond took a test in her home and paid a $6 fee in gold dust to obtain a certificate. With the help of an extra teacher, Mrs. Raymond was responsible for teaching 50 to 60 students each day out of the 81 students enrolled at the school. Sarah Raymond was paid at a rate of $125 per month, and Mrs. Sutter paid $75 per month. There were no books stored in the school. In their place was an assortment of books brought in by various emigrants. Sarah quit teaching the following year, but would later become the Madison County superintendent of schools.
