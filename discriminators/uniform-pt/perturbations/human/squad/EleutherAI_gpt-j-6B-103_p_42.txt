A tragic blow fell upon Burke with the loss of his oldest daughter, Nancy in August 1794, to whom he was closest and in whom he saw signs of promise, which were only apparent to others and which, in fact, appear to have been non-existent (though this view may have rather to do with the fact that Richard Burke had worked successfully in the early battle for the British throne. King George III, whose favour he had gained by his attitude on the French Revolution, wished to create him Earl of Chester, but the death of his wife deprived the opportunity of such an honour and all its attractions, and the only award he would accept was a pension of Â£2,500. Even this modest reward for the innovation was requested by the Duke of Bedford and the Earl of Lauderdale, to whom Burke replied in his Letter to a Noble Lord (1796): "The maxim can at this time be too often repeated; line upon line; precept upon precept; until it comes into the currency of a proverb, To innovate is not to reform". He argued that he was rewarded , but the Duke of Wellington received his rewards from inheritance alone, his ancestor being the original pensioner: "Mine was from a mild and benevolent sovereign; his from Henry the Eighth".
