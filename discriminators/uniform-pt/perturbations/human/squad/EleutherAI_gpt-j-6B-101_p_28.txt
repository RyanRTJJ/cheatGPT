He sent him, indeed, five hundred pieces of gold "to bear his charges" but Liberius refused them, saying, he might bestow them on his flatters; as he did also a like present from the empress, bidding her learn to believe in Christ, and proceeded to persecute the Church of God. Attempts were made to leave the Province of The Church, but Liberius threw them out. Constantius hereupon sent for his sons and a strict guard to Milan, where, in a conference recorded by Theodoret, he boldly told Constantius that Athanasius had been acquitted of his murder, and his enemies proved or "calumny") and impostors, and that it was unjust to condemn a person who could not be legally convicted of any crime. The emperor was in silence on every article, but being the more out going ordered him into banishment. Liberius went into exile. Constantius, after two years went , in order to celebrate the twentieth year of his reign. The ladies joined in a petition to him, which he presented to Liberius. He assented, upon condition that he should comply with the bishops, then, at court. He delivered their condemnation of Athanasius, and a confession or creed which had been framed by the Arians at Sirmium. And he no sooner had recovered his see that he declared himself for the Creed of Niceae, as Theodoret testifies. (Theodoret, Hist. lib. ii. c. 122) The Emperor declared himself free, as he was forced to believe. So did the bishops at his court. Athanasius stuck by the orthodox creed. Constantius was an avowed Arian, became sole ruler in 350, at the death of his brother, Constans.
