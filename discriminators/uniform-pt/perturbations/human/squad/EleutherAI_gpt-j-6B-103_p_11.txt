A tragic accident passed upon Burke with the loss of Richard in August , to whom he was tenderly attached, and in whom he saw signs of promise, which were not evident to others and which, in fact, appear to have been non-existent . This view may have rather reflected the fact that Richard Burke had worked successfully in the cause for Catholic emancipation). King George III, whose favour he had gained by his role in the French Revolution, wished to create him Earl of Beaconsfield, but the death of his son deprived the opportunity of such an honour and all its attractions, so the only award he received was a pension . Even this modest reward was the death penalty of the Duke of Bedford and the Earl of Lauderdale, to whom Burke replied in his Letter to the Lord (1796): "It cannot at this time be too often repeated; line upon line; precept upon precept; till it comes into the currency of a proverb, To reform is not to reform". He argued that he was rewarded on merit, but the Duke of Bedford received his rewards from inheritance alone, his ancestor being the sovereign; Burke commented: "Mine was from Henry, the benign and benevolent sovereign; his from Henry ."
