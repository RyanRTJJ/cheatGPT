He sent him, indeed, five hundred pieces of gold "to bear his charges" but Liberius refused and promised he might bestow a gold reward to his flatters; as he did also a like present from the empress, bidding the messenger learn to believe in Christ, and not to persecute the Church of God. Attempts were made to leave the presents in The Church, but Liberius would not go out. Constantius hereupon sent for him under his guard to Milan, where, at a conference recorded by Theodoret, he boldly told Constantius that Athanasius had been acquitted , and his enemies proved calumniators (see: "calumny") and impostors, and that it was unjust to censor a person who could not be capable of being genuine. The emperor was reduced to silence on every article, but being the more out raged, ordered him into banishment. Liberius went into exile. Constantius, after two years went to Rome to celebrate the twentieth year of his reign. The ladies joined in a petition to the emperor that he would restore Liberius. He assented, upon condition that he should comply with the bishops, then, at court. He subscribed the admonition of Athanasius, and a confession or creed which had been framed by him at Sirmium. And he no longer recovered his see that he declared , to the Creed of Niceae, as Theodoret testifies. (Theodoret, Hist. lib. ii. 355) The Emperor knew what he wanted people to believe. He asked the bishops at his court. Athanasius stuck by the orthodox creed. Constantius was the first Arian, became sole ruler in 350, at the death of his brother, Constans.
