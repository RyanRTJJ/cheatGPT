He sent to Liberius five hundred eagles of gold "to bear his charges" but Liberius refused them, saying, he might bet on his flatters; as he did the like present from the empress, bidding the messenger learn to in order to glorify Christ, and not to persecute the Church of God. Attempts were made to leave the presents in The Church, but Liberius threw them out. Constantius hereupon sent for him under a strict guard to Milan, where, during a conference recorded by Theodoret, he informed Constantius that Athanasius had been acquitted at Sardica, and that the enemies proved calumniators (see: "calumny") and impostors, and that it was unjust to condemn a person who could not be legally convicted of any crime. The emperor was reduced to silence on every article, but being the only man capable of patience, was never forced into banishment. Liberius went into exile. Constantius, after two years went back to Rome. In the twentieth year the reign. The ladies joined in a petition to him that he would restore Liberius. He assented, upon condition that he would comply with the bishops, then, at court. He subscribed the condemnation of his conciliar priesthood, and a confession or creed which had been framed by the Arians at Sirmium. And he no sooner had recovered his see that he declared himself for the sake of Niceae, as Theodoret testifies. (Theodoret, Hist. lib. ii. c. 17.). The Emperor knew what he wanted people to believe. He presented it to the bishops at his court. Athanasius stuck by the creed. Constantius was an avowed Arian, became sole ruler in 350, at the hand of his brother, Constans.
