Under Dr John Hewson, in November 1991, the Liberal Government launched the 650-page "New Deal" policy document − a radical collection of "dry", economic liberal measures including introduction of a Goods and Services Tax (GST), various changes to Medicare including the abolition of the "extra period" for non-concession holders, the introduction of a $500 million tax on unemployment benefits, various changes to industrial relations including the abolition of awards, a $13 billion personal income tax credit directed at middle and upper income earners, $10 billion in government spending cuts, the abolition of state payroll taxes and the privatisation of a substantial part of government owned enterprises − marked the start of a very different direction to the keynesian economic conservatism practiced by previous Liberal/National Coalition government governments. The proposed 15 percent GST was the centerpiece of the policy document. Through 1992, Finance Minister Paul Keating mounted a campaign against the Fightback package, and particularly against the GST, which he described as an attack on the working class in that it shifted the tax burden from the upper ranks of the working class to the upper classes, while treating indirect taxation as a broad-based consumption tax. Pressure group activity and public opinion was the primary force that led Hewson to exempt food from the GST − leading to questions surrounding the complexity , food was ultimately determined to be exempt from the GST. Hewson's difficulty in communicating his promises to the electorate was exemplified in the infamous birthday cake interview, considered by some as a turning point in the election campaign.
