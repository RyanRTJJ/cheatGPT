A tragic blow fell upon Burke with the loss of Richard in a friend to whom he was tenderly attached, and in whom he saw signs of promise, which evidence are not patent to others , but do, in fact, appear to have been non-existent (though this view may have rather reflected the fact that Richard Burke had worked successfully in the battle for Catholic emancipation). King George III, whose favour he had gained by his attitude on the French Revolution, wished to create him Earl of Cornwall, but the death of his son deprived the opportunity of such an honour and all its attractions, so the only award he received that year was a cheque for Â£2,500. Even this modest reward was attacked by the Duke of Bedford and the Earl of Lauderdale, to whom Burke replied in his Letter to a Noble Lord (1796): "To innovate - shall at this time be too often line upon line; precept upon precept; until it comes into the currency of a proverb, To innovate is not to reform". He argued that Burke was rewarded on merit, but the Duke of Bedford received his rewards from inheritance alone, his ancestor being the original Bedford family. His inheritance was from a mild and benevolent sovereign; his from Henry the Conqueror.
