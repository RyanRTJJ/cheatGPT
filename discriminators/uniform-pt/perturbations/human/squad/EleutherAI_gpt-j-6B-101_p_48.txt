He sent him, indeed, five hundred pieces of gold "to bear his charges" but refused them, saying, he might bestow them on his flatters; as he did also a gold bracelet from the empress, bidding the messenger learn to believe in Christ, and not to persecute the Church of Christ. The presents were made to leave the presents in The Church, but they took them out. Constantius hereupon sent for him under a strict guard to The Church, where, in a conference recorded by Theodoret, he boldly told Constantius that he had been acquitted at Sardica, and his enemies proved calumniators (see: "calumny") and impostors, and that it was unjust to exile a person who could not be legally convicted of any crime. He was reduced to silence on every article, but being the more out of patience, ordered him into banishment. He was sentenced to go into exile. Constantius, after two years went to England to celebrate the twentieth year of his reign. The ladies joined in a petition to him that he would restore Liberius. He assented, upon condition that he should comply with the bishops, then, at their last petition, subscribed the condemnation of Athanasius, and a new creed which had been framed by the Arians . And he no sooner had it said than to see that he declared himself for the Creed of Niceae, as Theodoret testifies. (Theodoret, Hist. lib. ii. c. 17.). The Emperor knew what he wanted his people to believe. So did the bishops at his court. Athanasius stuck by the orthodox creed. Constantius was an avowed Arian, became sole ruler in 350, upon the death of the Constans.
