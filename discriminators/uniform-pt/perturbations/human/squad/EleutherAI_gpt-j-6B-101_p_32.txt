He gave Liberius, indeed, five hundred pieces of gold "to bear his charges" but Liberius refused them, hoping he might bestow them on his flatters; as he did also a like gift to the empress, bidding the messenger learn to believe in Christ, and not to persecute the Church of God. Attempts were made to leave him in The Church, but Liberius threw them out. Constantius hereupon sent him under a strict guard to Milan, where, in a conference with Theodoret, he boldly told Constantius that Athanasius had been acquitted at the cross, and therefore his enemies proved calumniators and even as thieves and impostors, and that it was unjust to condemn a person who could not be legally convicted of any crime. The emperor was inclined toward silence on some points, but being the more out of patience, ordered him into banishment. Liberius went into exile. Constantius, after two years went to Rome to celebrate the twentieth year of his reign. The ladies joined in a petition to him that he would restore Liberius. He assented, upon condition that it should be done not in the same manner at court. He subscribed the condemnation of Athanasius, and a confession or creed which had been framed by some at Sirmium. And he no sooner had recovered his see that he declared himself for the Creed of Niceae, as Theodoret testifies. (Theodoret, Hist. lib. V, p 17.). The Emperor knew what he wanted people to believe. He did the same. Constantius and his government stuck by the orthodox creed. Constantius was an avowed Arian, became sole ruler in 350, at the death of his brother, Constans.
