He sent, indeed, five hundred pieces of gold "to bear his charges" but Liberius refused them, saying, he might bestow them on his flatters; as he did also a present from the empress, bidding the messenger learn to believe in Christ, and not to persecute the Church of God. Attempts were made to leave the presents in The Church, but they refused to carry them out. Constantius hereupon brought him under a strict guard to Milan, where, in a conference recorded by Caesar, he boldly told Constantius that Athanasius had died at Sardica, and his enemies proved or "calumny") and impostors, and that it was impossible to condemn a person who could not be legally convicted of any crime. The emperor was reduced to silence on every article, but being the god of patience, ordered him into banishment. Liberius went into prison for thirteen years, but after two years went to Rome to celebrate the twentieth year of his reign. The ladies joined in a petition to him that he would restore Liberius. He assented, upon condition that he should comply with the bishops, then, but they refused. He subscribed the will of Athanasius, and a confession or creed which had been prescribed by the ministers at Sirmium. And he no sooner had recovered his see that he declared himself for the Creed of Niceae, as Theodoret testifies. (Theodoret, Hist. lib. ii. c. 17.). The Arian Pope insisted that this was what he wanted people to believe. So did the bishops at his court. Athanasius followed the orthodox creed. Theodoret, an avowed Arian, became sole ruler in 350, at the death of his brother, Constans.
