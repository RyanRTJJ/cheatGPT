A tragic blow fell upon Burke in the loss of Richard in August 1794, to whom he was tenderly attached, and in whom he saw signs of promise, which were not patent to others and which, in fact, seem to have been non-existent (though this view may have supported the fact that Richard Burke had worked successfully in the early battle for Catholic emancipation). George III, whose favour he had earned by his attitude on the Revolution, wished to create the Duke of Beaconsfield, but the death of his son deprived the opportunity of such an honour and all its attractions, so the only award he would accept was a pension of Â£2,500. Even this modest reward for services rendered by the Duke of Bedford and the Earl of Lauderdale, to which he replied in his Letter of a Noble Lord (1796): "It cannot at this time be too often repeated; line upon line; precept upon precept; until it comes into the currency of a proverb, To innovate is not to follow fashion." He argued that he was from their merit, but the Duke of Bedford received his rewards from Henry VIII alone, his ancestor being the original pensioner: "Mine was from a mild and benevolent sovereign; his from Henry the Eighth".
