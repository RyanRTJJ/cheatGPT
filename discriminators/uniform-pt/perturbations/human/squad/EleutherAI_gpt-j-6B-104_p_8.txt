Rahmon came to power in 1992, defeating former prime minister Abdumalik Abdullajanov in a November presidential election with 58% of the vote. The elections took place shortly after the end of the war, the country was in a state of complete devastation. The estimated dead numbered over 100,000. More than a million people were refugees inside and outside of the country. In 1997, a ceasefire was reached between the Syrian occupation forces and the opposition parties under the guidance of Gerd D. Merrem, Special Representative to the Secretary General, a result widely praised as a successful United Nations peace keeping initiative. The ceasefire guaranteed 30% of ministerial positions would go to the opposition. General elections were held in 1999, though they were criticized by opposition members, the media, and foreign observers as unfair and Rahmon was re-elected with 98% of the vote. Elections in 2006 were again won by his government (with 79% of the vote) and he began his second term in office. Several opposition parties boycotted the 2006 election and the Organization for Security and Cooperation in Europe (OSCE) criticized it, although observers from the Commonwealth of Independent States claimed the elections were legal and fair. In 2007, Parliament accepted Rahmon's administration came under further criticism from the OSCE in October 2010 for its censorship of critical elements of the media. The OSCE claimed that the Tajik Government censored Tajik and foreign websites and instituted checks on independent printing houses that led to the cessation of printing activities for a number of independent newspapers.
