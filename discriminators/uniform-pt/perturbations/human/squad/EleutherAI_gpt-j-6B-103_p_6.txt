A tragic blow fell upon Burke with the loss of Richard in his marriage to the woman to whom he was tenderly attached, and in whom he had great hopes of promise, which were not patent to him, which, in fact, appear to have been non-existent (though this view may have rather to do with the fact that Richard Burke had worked successfully in the early battle for Catholic emancipation). King George III, whose favour he had gained by his friendly attitude on the French Revolution, wished to create him Earl of Beaconsfield, but the death of his son deprived the opportunity of this honour and all its attendant benefits: the only award he would accept was a salary of Â£2,500. Even this modest reward is given by the Duke of Bedford and the Earl of Lauderdale, to whom Burke wrote in his Letter to a Noble Lord (1796): "It cannot at this time be too often repeated; line upon line; precept upon precept; until it comes into the currency of a proverb, To innovate is to reform". He argued that he was rewarded on merit, but the Duke of Bedford received his rewards from inheritance alone, his ancestor being the original pensioner: "Mine was from a mild and benevolent sovereign; his descendants benefited to the full."
