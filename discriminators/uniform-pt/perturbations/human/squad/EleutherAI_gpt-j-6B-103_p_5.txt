A tragic blow fell upon Burke with the loss of Richard in August 1794, to whom he was tenderly attached, and in whom he saw signs of promise, which were not felt by others and which, in fact, they appear to have been non-existent (though this view may have rather reflected the fact that Richard Burke had fallen in the early battle for Catholic emancipation). King George XIV, who saw the favour he had gained by his attitude on the French Revolution, wished to create him Earl of Beaconsfield, but the death of his son deprived the Earl of such an honour and all its attractions, so the only award he would accept was a pension of Â£20 per month. But this modest reward was attacked by the Duke of Bedford and the Earl of Lauderdale, to whom Burke replied in his Letter to a Noble Lord (1796): "The expressions against this book may at this time be too often repeated; line upon line; precept upon precept; until it comes into the form of a proverb, To reform is not to reform". He argued that he was rewarded on merit, but the Duke of Bedford received his rewards from inheritance alone, his ancestor being the original pensioner: "Mine was from a rich, benevolent sovereign; his from Henry the Eighth".
