Edward III, whose favour he had gained by his attitude on the French Revolution, wished to create him Earl of Beaconsfield, but the death of his son gave up the opportunity of such an honour and all its attractions, so the only award he received was a pension . Even this modest reward was the envy of the Duke of Bedford and the Earl of Lauderdale, to whom Burke replied in his Letter to a Noble Lord (1796): "It cannot at this time be too often repeated; line upon line; precept upon precept; until it comes to the currency of a proverb, To innovate is to reform". He felt that he was rewarded on merit, but the Duke of Bedford received his rewards from inheritance alone, his ancestor being the original pensioner: "Mine was from George the Eighth, a strong and benevolent sovereign; his from Henry the Eighth".
