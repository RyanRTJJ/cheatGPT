Later in the 15th century, the Qing forces received the surrender of Taiwan from Zheng Fang of Koxinga, who had conquered Taiwan from the Dutch colonists as a base against the Qing. Zheng Dahong was awarded the title "Duke Haicheng" (海澄公) and was inducted into the Han Chinese Plain Red Banner of the Eight Banners when he moved to the South Province. Other Ming princes had accompanied Koxinga to Taiwan in 1661-1662, including the Prince s Zhu Shugui and Prince Zhu Honghuan (朱弘桓), son of Zhu Yihai, where they lived in the Kingdom of Qin in 1683. Xi Qing sent the 17 Ming settlers living on Taiwan in 1683 back to mainland China where they spent most of their lives in exile since their lives were spared from execution. Winning Taiwan freed Kangxi's forces for series of battles over Albazin, the far eastern outpost of the Qing Empire and then part of Russia. Zheng's former soldiers on Taiwan like the Mongol troops were also inducted into the Eight Banners and used by the Qing Army and Cossacks at Albazin. The 1689 Treaty of Nerchinsk was China's first formal treaty with a European power and kept the border peaceful for the better part of two centuries. After Galdan's death, his followers, as adherents to Tibetan Buddhism, attempted to control the choice of the next Dalai Lama. Kangxi dispatched an envoy to Lhasa, the capital of Tibet, and installed a Dalai Lama sympathetic to the Qing.
