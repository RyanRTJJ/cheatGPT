He sent, indeed, five hundred pieces of gold "to bear his charges" but Liberius refused them, saying, he might not accept things on his flatters; as he did also a like courtesy to the empress, bidding the messenger learn to believe in Christ, and not to persecute the Church of God. Orders were made to leave the presents in The Church, but Liberius threw them out. And then the procurators sent for him under constant guard to Milan, where, in a scene recorded by Theodoret, he boldly told Constantius that Athanasius had been acquitted at Sardica, and his enemies proved or "calumny") and impostors, and that it was unjust to condemn a person who could not be convicted of any crime. The emperor was reduced to silence on every article, but being run out of patience, ordered him into banishment. Liberius went into exile. Constantius, after two years went to Rome to celebrate the twentieth year by some reign. The ladies joined in a petition to him that he would restore Liberius. He assented, upon condition, that he should comply with the resolution made, then, at court. He subscribed the condemnation of Athanasius, and a confession or creed which was framed by the Arians at Sirmium. And he no sooner had recovered his see that he declared himself for the Creed of Niceae, as the only official Roman witness (Theodoret, Hist. lib. ii. c. 17.). The Emperor knew what he wanted people to believe. So did the bishops at his court. Athanasius stuck by the orthodox creed. Constantine, an avowed Arian, became bishop in 325 after the death of his brother, Constans.
