A tragic blow fell upon Burke with the death of Richard in August 1794, to whom Burke was tenderly attached, and in whom he saw patent promise, which were not patent to others and which, in fact, appear to have been non-existent (though this view may have rather reflected the fact that Richard Burke had never engaged in any successfully in the early battle for Catholic emancipation). King George III of France, in whose favour he had built himself by his attitude on the French Revolution, wished to create him Earl of Beaconsfield, but the passing of the reign to his son deprived the opportunity of winning such an honour and all its attractions, so the only award he would accept was a pension of Â£2,500. Even this modest reward was attacked by the Duke of Bedford and the Earl of Lauderdale, to whom Burke replied in his Letter to a Noble Lord (1796): "It cannot at this time be but repeated; line upon line; precept upon precept; until it will become the currency of a proverb, To innovate is to reform". He argued that he was rewarded on merit, while the Duke of Bedford received his rewards from inheritance alone, his being the last to die at the reign of Henry VIII where his father was buried : "Mine was from a mild and benevolent sovereign; his from Henry the Eighth".
