He sent him, indeed, five hundred pieces of gold "to bear his charges" but Liberius refused them: saying, he might bestow them on flatters; as he did also a like present from the empress, bidding the messenger learn to believe in Christ, and not to persecute the people of God. The men were also made to leave the building of The Church, but Liberius threw them out. Constantius hereupon sent for him under a strict guard to Milan, where, in a conference recorded by Theodoret, he boldly told Constantius that Athanasius and his followers had been acquitted at Sardica, and his enemies proved calumniators (see: "calumny") and impostors, and that it was unjust to kill a person who could not be legally convicted of any crime. The emperor was reduced to silence on every article, but being the more out of patience, he went into a depression and went into exile. Constantius, after two years went to Rome to celebrate the twentieth year of his reign. The ladies joined in a petition to him (which he would accept). He assented, upon condition that he should comply with the bishops, then, at court. He received a letter of condemnation of Athanasius, and a confession or creed which had been framed by the Arians at that time, so he no sooner had recovered his see that he declared himself for the Creed of Niceae, as Rome testifies. (Theodoret, Hist. lib. p. 17.). The Emperor decreed what he wanted people to believe. So Athanasius kept the bishops at his court. Athanasius stuck by the orthodox creed. Constantius was an avowed Arian, became sole ruler in 350, at the death of his brother, Constans.
