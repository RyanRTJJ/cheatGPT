He sent him, indeed, five hundred pieces of gold "to bear his charges" but Liberius refused them, wishing he might bestow them on him just as he had given a like present from the empress, bidding the messenger learn to believe in Christ, and not to persecute the Church of God. Attempts were made to leave the presents in The Church, but Liberius threw them : Constantius hereupon sent for him under a disguise to Milan, where, at a conference recorded by Theodoret, he boldly told Constantius that Athanasius had been acquitted of all charges: and his enemies proved calumniators (see: "calumny") and impostors, and that it was unjust to condemn a person who could not be legally convicted of any crime. The emperor was reduced to silence on every article, but when no more evidence showed his patience, ordered him into banishment. Liberius went into exile. Constantius, after having been exiled, went to Rome to celebrate the twentieth year of his reign. The ladies joined in a pledge to him that he would restore the see; he assented, upon condition that he should comply with the bishops, then, at court. He subscribed the condemnation of the church, and a confession or creed which had been framed by the Arians at Sirmium. And he no doubt recovered his see that he declared himself for the Creed of Niceae, as Theodoret testifies. (Theodoret, Hist. lib. ii. c. 17.). The odoret declared what he came to believe. So did the bishops at his court. Athanasius stuck to the orthodox creed. Constantius was an avowed Arian, became bishop in 350, at the death of his brother, Constans.
