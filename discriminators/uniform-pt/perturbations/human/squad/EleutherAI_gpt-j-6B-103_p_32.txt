King George III, whose favour he had gained by his attitude on the French Revolution, wished to create him Baron of Beaconsfield, but the death of his son deprived the opportunity of such an honour and all its attractions, so the only award he was given was a pension of Â£2,500. Even this modest reward was attacked by the Duke of Wellington and the Earl of Lauderdale, to whom Burke replied in his Letter to a Noble Lord (1796): "It cannot at this time be so repeated; line upon line; precept after precept until it comes into the currency of a proverb, To innovate is not to innovate." The Duke of Wellington argued that he was rewarded on merit, but the Duke of Bedford received his rewards from inheritance alone, despite being the original pensioner: Bedford "got his pension from a mild and benevolent sovereign; his from Henry the Eighth".
