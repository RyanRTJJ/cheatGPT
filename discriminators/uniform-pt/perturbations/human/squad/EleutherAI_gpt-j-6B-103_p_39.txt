A tragic blow fell upon Burke with the loss of Richard in August 1794, to whom he was tenderly attached, and in whom he saw signs of qualities which were not patent to others and which, in Burke’s view, were more evident to have been that of himself than of others; and this may have rather reflected the fact that Richard Burke had worked successfully in the early battle for Catholic emancipation). King George III, whose favour he had gained by his attitude on the French Revolution, wished to create him Earl of Accrington, but the death of his son prevented him the opportunity of such an honour and all its attractions, so the only award he would accept was a pension of £2,500. Even this modest reward was attacked by the Duke of Bedford and the Earl of Worcester. To this he wrote to the Earl of Leicester and replied in his Letter to a Noble Lord (1796): "It cannot at this time be too often repeated; line upon line; precept upon precept; until it comes into the force of a proverb, To innovate is not to reform". He argued that he was a title through military merit, but the Duke of Bedford received the crown from inheritance alone, his ancestor being the Duke of Cornwall. "Mine was from a mild and benevolent sovereign; his from Henry the Eighth".
