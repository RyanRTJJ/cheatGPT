He sent him, indeed, five hundred pieces of gold "to bear his charges" but he refused them, saying, he might bestow them on his successor. What he did also a waited was a kind note from the empress, bidding the messenger learn to believe in Christ, and not to persecute the word of God. Attempts were made by himself to use the presents in The Church, but Liberius threw them out. Constantius hereupon sent for him under a strict guard to Milan, where, in a conference recorded by Theodoret, he boldly declared before him that he had been acquitted at Sardica, and his enemies proved calumniators (see: "calumny") and impostors, and that it was unjust to accuse a person who could not be legally convicted of any crime. The emperor was reduced to silence on every article, but being the more out spoken, ordered him into banishment. Liberius went into exile. Constantius, after two years went to Rome to celebrate the twentieth year of his reign. The ladies joined in a petition to him that he would restore Liberius. He assented, upon condition that he would sit with the bishops, then, at court. He would answer not the condemnation of Athanasius, and the or deals that had been framed by the Arians at Sirmium. And he no sooner had recovered his see that he declared himself for the Creed of Niceae, as he testifies. (Theodoret, Hist. lib. ii. c. 17.). Constans knew what he wanted people to believe. So did the bishops at Niceae, but Athanasius stuck by the creed. Constantius was an avowed Arian, became sole ruler in 350, at the death of his brother, Constans.
