A tragic blow fell upon Burke with the loss of Richard in August 1794, to whom he was tenderly attached, and in whom he had found the means of obtaining inventions which were not patent to others and which, in Burkes view, ought to have been rather unusual, this view may have rather reflected the fact that Richard Burke had worked successfully in the early battle for Catholic emancipation). King George III, whose favour he gained by his attitude on the French Revolution, wished to create the Duke of Beaconsfield, but the death of his son s halted the opportunity of such an honour and all its attractions, so the only award he would have received was a title to a pension of Â£2,500. Even this modest reward was given by the Duke of Bedford and the Earl of Lauderdale, to whom Burke replied in A Letter to a Noble Lord (1796): "It cannot at this time be repeated, but it will be repeated; line upon line; precept upon precept; until it comes into the currency of a proverb, To innovate is to reform". He argued that he was rewarded on a pension, the Duke of Bedford received his rewards from inheritance alone, instead of being the original pensioner: "Mine was from the kindly and benevolent sovereign; his from Henry the great".
