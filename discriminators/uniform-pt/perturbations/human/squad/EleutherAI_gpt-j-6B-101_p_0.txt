He sent him, indeed, five hundred pieces of cloth "to bear his charges" but Liberius refused them, saying, he might bestow their gifts upon his flatters; as he did also the present from the empress, bidding the messenger learn to believe in Christ, and not to persecute the Church of Rome, they were made to leave their property in The Church, but Constantius forced them out. Constantius hereupon sent for him under a strict guard to Milan, where, in a conference recorded in the Library of Milan, he boldly told Constantius that Athanasius had been acquitted at Sardica, and his enemies proved calumniators (see: "calumny") and impostors, and that it was better to execute a person who could not be legally convicted of any crime. The emperor was reduced to silence on these charges, but being the more out of patience, ordered him into banishment. The church leader was sent into exile. Constantius, after two years went to Rome to celebrate the twentieth year of his reign. The council requested in a petition to him that he would restore Liberius. This was upon condition that he should comply with the bishops, according to the rules of his court. He subscribed the condemnation of Athanasius, and retracted the confession or creed which had been framed by the Arians at Sirmium. And he no sooner had recovered his see that he declared himself for the Creed of Niceae, as Theodoret testifies. (Theodoret, Hist. lib. ii. c. 17.). Constans knew what he wanted to believe. So did the bishops at his court. Athanasius stuck by the orthodox creed. Constantius , though avowed Arian, became sole ruler in 350, at the death of the other Constans.
