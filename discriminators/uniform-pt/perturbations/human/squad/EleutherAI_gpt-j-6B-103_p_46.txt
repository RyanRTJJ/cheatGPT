A tragic event upon Burke was the loss of Richard in August 1794, to whom he was tenderly attached, and in whom he had made treasuries of promise, which were not patent to others and which, in fact, appear to have been non-existent (though this view may have been influenced by the fact that Richard Burke had worked successfully in the early battle for Catholic emancipation). King George III, whose favour he had gained by his attitude on the French Revolution, wished to grant the title of Earl of Buckingham, but the death of his son deprived the opportunity of such an honour and all its attractions, so the only position he would accept was a pension of Â£2,500. Even this modest reward was awarded the Duke of Bedford and the Earl of Lauderdale, to whom Burke replied in his Letter to a Noble Lord (1796): "It cannot at this time be too often repeated; line upon line; precept upon precept; until it comes into the currency of a proverb, To innovate is not to reform". He argued that this was rewarded financially, but the Duke of Bedford received his rewards from inheritance alone, his ancestor being the original pensioner: His pension came from a mild and benevolent sovereign; his from Henry the Fourth's cruelness and cruelty.
