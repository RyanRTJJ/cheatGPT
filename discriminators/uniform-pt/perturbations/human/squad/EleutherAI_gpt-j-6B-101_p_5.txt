He sent him, indeed, five hundred pieces of gold "to bear " but Liberius refused them, saying, he might bestow them on him, as he did also a wand from the empress, bidding the messenger learn to of Christ, and to persecute the Church of God. Attempts were made to leave the presents in The Church, but Liberius threw them out. Constantius hereupon sent for him under his guard to Milan, where, in a conference recorded by Theodoret, he boldly told Constantius that Athanasius had been martyred at Sardica, and his enemies proved calumniators (see: apostases), that they were impostors, and that it was incriminating to condemn a person who could not be legally convicted of any crime. The emperor was reduced to silence on every article, but being the more out of patience, ordered him to retire. Liberius went into poverty and after two years went to Rome to celebrate the twentieth year from their reign. The ladies sent a petition to him that he would restore Liberius. He assented, upon condition that he should meet with the bishops, then, at court. He subscribed the condemnation of Athanasius, and a confession or creed which had been framed by the bishops of Sirmium. And he no sooner had recovered his see that he declared himself for the Creed of Niceae, as Theodoret testifies. (De lib. ii. c. 17.). The Emperor knew what he wanted people to believe. So did the bishops and the royal court. Athanasius stuck by the orthodox y. Constantine, who was an avowed Arian, became sole ruler in 350, at the death of his brother, Constans.
