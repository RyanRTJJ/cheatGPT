A tragic blow fell upon Burke by the loss of Richard Morrison in 1794, to whom he was tenderly attached, and in whom he saw signs of promise, which were not patent to others and , in fact, appear to have been non-existent (though this may have rather reflected the fact that Richard Burke had worked successfully in the early battle for Catholic emancipation). This argument was contested by the Duke of Bedford and the Earl of Lauderdale, to whom Burke replied in his Letter to a Noble Lord (1796): "It cannot at such a time be too much; it must be line upon line; precept upon precept; until it comes into the spirit of a proverb, To innovate is not to reform". He argued that he was rewarded on merit, but the Duke of Bedford of Gloucester derived his rewards from inheritance alone, his ancestor being the original pensioner: "Mine was from a mild and benevolent sovereign; his from Henry the Eighth".
