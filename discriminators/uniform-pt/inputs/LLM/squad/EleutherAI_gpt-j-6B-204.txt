The city has a population of more than 5 million, and its geographical location, the Yangtze River, was a major economic and military force for all of Imperial China. At one point during the Yuan dynasty (1279–1368), Nanjing was the largest city in the world, with a population of more than 1.8 million. At the time, the city was divided into thirty-eight districts. There were ten palaces (including the Forbidden City) and over 2,000 Buddhist temples, with about 200,000 people per temple. During the Ming dynasty (1368–1644), the name "Nanjing" was used to refer to both the city of the Ming dynasty and the province of Jiangsu, which in turn became its new provincial capital.

Contents

During the Tang dynasty (ad 618–907), Nanjing, located in the south of the Chang Jiang (Yangtze River), was a county in the Dong Guofan command of Jiangxi Province, then a part of the Sixteen Kingdoms [7]. Emperor Wu of Tang (r. 605–618) gave the name "nan zheng" (meaning literally "south capital") to the new capital city of Nanjing because of its location between the provinces of Jiangxi and Zhejiang. Emperor Xuanzong changed the name to "Guodong", which means "capital of the east", with reference to his intention to move the capital to Chang'an or Chang'anfu (present day Xi'an).
