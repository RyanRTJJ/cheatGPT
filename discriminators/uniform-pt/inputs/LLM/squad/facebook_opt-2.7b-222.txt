In 2012, Libya agreed to join the Rome Statute of the International Criminal Court.

In June 2015, former U.S. President Bill Clinton, U.N. Secretary-General Ban Ki-moon and U.N. High Commissioner for Human Rights Navi Pillay arrived in Libya to meet with Libyan leader Muammar Gaddafi in order to restart diplomatic efforts between the government and rebels attempting to topple him. Later that month, Russia's Foreign Ministry said that the Libyan government and rebels had agreed on a cease-fire. Two months later, the U.N. Special Representative of the Secretary-General for Libya announced that Gaddafi agreed to an arms embargo on the country.

In 2015, the United States lifted most sanctions against Libya under a so-called "Libyan Settlement Support Agreement" in April 2015.

In 2016, Libya's oil production capacity surpassed 1 million bpd and is estimated by the U.S. Energy Information Administration to reach 2.4 million bpd by 2018.

From late summer of 2016 to the start of spring 2017, a civil war broke out, with Libya's two governments competing for control of the country. The war came to a formal end in June 2017 with UN brokered talks between the two parties in Switzerland, the outcomes of which have not yet been implemented. On 15 July 2017, the Presidential Council of Libya announced the Government of National Accord as the winner of the Libyan political crisis.
