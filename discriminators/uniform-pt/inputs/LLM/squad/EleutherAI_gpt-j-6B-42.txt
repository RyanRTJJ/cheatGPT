The Israeli government announced in 1992 that it would make Hebrew an official language, in addition to English and Arabic. The move came as a result of the high population density of Israeli Jews and the need for Israeli Jewish citizens to function in a multicultural society. An education ministry decision in June of 1992 said that Hebrew would be added to the list of languages taught in Jewish day schools and Jewish schools.

The Israeli government is encouraging the use of Hebrew as there are more than 1.8 million Jews worldwide who learned Hebrew as their first language; according to the 1998 Jewish Community Survey by the U.S. Census Bureau, the percentage of Jews in the U.S. who speak Hebrew at home has tripled from 5.2 percent in 1965 to 15.7 percent in 1993.

“Hebrew has always played a central role in Jewish life,” says Jeffrey C. Kupfer, a professor of Jewish history at Hebrew Union College-Jewish Institute of Religion in Los Angeles, California. “The Torah is written in Hebrew. Many synagogues and Jewish organizations in Israel are in Hebrew. The rabbis and elders of major Jewish communities in the U.S. are the heads and senior clergy of Jewish congregations, and at some of them, they are the only clergy. In Jewish education in the U.S., the most common Hebrew I see is in the curriculum, not on the lips of teachers…. In the U.S., the Jewish world is primarily non-Semitic.”
