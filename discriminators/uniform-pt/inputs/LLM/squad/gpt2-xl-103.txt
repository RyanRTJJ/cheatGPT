The new Government was led by the Whig leader William Gladstone, a friend of William Pitt and a former attorney general when his father succeeded Gladstone as the Prime Minister in 1834.

In April 1836, Gladstone was offered the post of President of the Board of Trade, but he rejected the offer. In June, Gladstone was appointed Chancellor of the Exchequer, with Sir Edward Bulwer-Lytton, who had been his Secretary of State for War at a similar position before 1831, as Vice-Chancellor. In November 1836, Gladstone was appointed Prime Minister by the King. Gladstone was an advocate of commercial treaties, and his first year in office turned out to be one of significant success. All three major treaties negotiated by Gladstone's government were made with the United States.

After several years of success, however, Gladstone's government was forced to resign upon the outbreak of the Napoleonic wars in 1814. In 1818, as he was preparing to return to the office of Chancellor of the Exchequer, Gladstone was shot at the entrance of his carriage, wounding his left leg. Bulwer-Lytton was made Minister of the Navy with power to sign orders that would place the government on the warpath in the United States.
